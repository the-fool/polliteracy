Sample Size Experiment

Below is a random scattering of two kinds of dots -- red and blue. The true population proportion between the two is 1/1 (which would appear as 100%).

This is an experimental lab. The point is to make random samples, and see what results you get! Do not try to force your sample to be perfect -- it is supposed to random, as if you were blind!

Instructions:

  1. Click and drag a random rectangle.
  2. This is your sample. Notice the size and its sampled accuracy (perfectly accurate samples have a reb-blue ratio at 100%)
  3. Drag the rectangle around the population, making many random samples as you do
  4. Notice that most random samples stay fairly accurate (near 100%)
  5. Now experiment with different sample sizes -- notice that small samples can be quite accurate by chance! And notice that large samples are not much more accurate than small samples!
  6. Try to make your sample extremely wrong -- notice how difficult it is!
  7. Success! You now have an intuitive sense for why random sampling is accurate even with small sample sizes
Explanation
External Resources Link

What we see with this exercise is an application of the central limit theorem. This theorem is what lies behind the famous "bell-curve" of statistics.

It is basically the obvious assertion that with larger and larger sample sizes from the same population, the characteristics of these samples convere on a "central" point. Each sample can be slightly different from each other, but as they get larger these differences start to vanish. Hence, there is a "limit" at which those differences finally go down to zero. The "limit" here at the center is a perfect ratio of 1/1 (100%).

You can experiment with this by making a large box and dragging it around. Notice that the ratio hardly changes at all with each new sample!
