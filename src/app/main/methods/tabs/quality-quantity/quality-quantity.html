Sample Size and Sample Bias

Was it just bad luck that The Literary Digest predicted extremely inaccurate results? Sometimes it is just a matter of luck -- but not so here. The reason The Literary Digest was wrong is that same for why Gallup's poll was correct. The issue at stake here is what we term sample bias.

A sample is biased if it leans in one direction, favoring certain results, rather than representing a genuinely random (or unbiased) segment of the population. If a poll sample only gets responses from a specific type of member of some population, it does not matter whether the sample size is one-thousand or one-million -- in either case the sample will be unrepresentative of the population as a whole.

The problem with The Literary Digest was that their poll was biased in favor of wealthy, educated citizens (remember, they got their list of potential subjects from car registration forms and telephone directories). Their actual sample was mainly composed of people who met at least some of the following criteria:

  • Owned a registered automobile
  • Owned a telephone line
  • Subscribed to magazines
  • Paid dues as a member of certain elite clubs
  • Possessed the time, incentive, and money to mail back a questionnaire

Even though their sample size was humongous, their poll was biased enough to under-represent the amount of pro-Roosevelt sentiment in the poorer segments of the United States.

Check Answer Explanation

Imagine you have a large sack of beans. You scoop out a handful and find that they are all black. Then you scoop out two handfuls and see that they are all black, too. The next three handfuls are all black again. Do you see any reason to continue counting beans?

But what if I told you that you were only scooping out beans from the top of the bag? And at the bottom, most of the settled beans are red. In this case, your sample size was large, but it was biased.
