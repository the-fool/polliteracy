Sample Size and Population Size

When we poll, we are interested in knowing the true situation for an entire group. It is usually not possible to ask everyone in a large group for their opinion on an issue. If wee did, then we would know the exact statistical facts for the entire population. We would not be mistaken, since we did not leave anyone out.

But, since it's not feasible to poll an entire population, we try to find ways to get a reasonably good guess about the entire group from polling a small portion of them. This piece of the population that actually gets polled is called the sample.

One goal of the scientists who make polls is then to get the most accurate information about a population without needing to poll an impractically large sample of the population.

How Big is Big Enough?

If we only poll a subset of a population, there is a chance that we missed some crucial information. Our poll could be wrong! How should we minimize the chance for error?

One simple fix is to increase the sample size! But then we need to ask ourselves: how big is big enough? It costs time and money to poll people! So we hope to strike a balance between the costs of sampling a large amount of people and the better accuracy with our poll results.

This balance is a very important question, and it has been subject to a whole field of research. Today, scientists have reached a consensus on how large a sample size needs to be in order to be a reasonably good representation of the population. It turns out, a sample less than 1000 can be good for all of the USA!
