Sample Size and Population Size

When we poll, we are interested in knowing the true situation for an entire area. It is usually not possible to ask everyone in a large group for their opinion on an issue. But what If we did? If we gathered up everyone, and asked them for their opinion on an issue, then we would pretty much know the exact statistical make-up of the entire population. We would not be mistaken, since we did not leave anyone out.

But, since it's not easy to poll an entire population, we try to find ways to get a reasonably good guess about the entire group from our merely polling a portion of them. This piece of the population that actually gets polled is called the sample. One goal of the scientists who make polls is then to get the most accurate information about a population without needing to poll an impractically large sample of the population.

How big is big enough?

If we only poll a subset of a population, there is a chance that we missed some crucial information. This means that our poll could be wrong -- but it could also be right. How should we minimize the chance for error?

One obvious answer is just to increase the sample size! But then we need to ask ourselves: how big is big enough? It costs time and money to poll people! So we hope to strike a balance between the costs of sampling a large amount of people and the better accuracy with our poll results.

This balance is a very important question, and it has been subject to a whole field of research. Today, scientists have reached a general consensus on the issue over how large a sample size needs to be in order to be a reasonably good representation of the population. We will get to their conclusions soon, but first how about you take a guess?
