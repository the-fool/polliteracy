Early Reports

Over the years since the fiasco with The Literary Digest, polling methodology has gotten better and better. With this increase in our skill at conducting polls comes an ability to forecast the likely outcome of events before they run their full course. It is now a commonplace that large news organizations forecast the end result of a presidential race, well before most votes have been counted.

Pollsters may predict the final outcome ahead of time with a variety of tools and techniques. One, which we have already seen, is the probability that a small sample, if it is sufficiently random, will be near the truth. Sometimes, the way they gather their sample on the day of an election is through a process called exit polling. This is so-named because the sample is collected from people as the exit their polling booths.

The ethics of early reporting

What effect could this ability to predict elections have on our political process? It is exciting to get early predictions -- but the early forecasts can also cause problems for the voters. Imagine that you are waiting in line to cast your vote, and then you see on your phone that your favored candidate has already "lost" according to the exit-polls. Would you stay in line and cast your vote anyway?

One of the most dramatic incidents of this early forecasting in the presidential race was in the 1980 contest between Reagan and Carter. NBC News predicted Reagan's victory at 8:15pm EST, well before the West Coast polling stations even closed for the day! This was the earliest forecast ever, and this incident sent a shockwave through the media. Nowadays, news stations make a promise not to call a winner before voting officially ends, since they do not want to depress voter turnout.

Exploration

There is no right or wrong answer to this question, and indeed there have been many lawsuits from both sides of the issue. On the one hand, people should have a right to broadcast facts, but on the other hand, people should be able to vote in an undisturbed environment. The difficulty with resolving this issue seems to stem from the way in which voting is a deeply private affair, while polling is a purely public act.
