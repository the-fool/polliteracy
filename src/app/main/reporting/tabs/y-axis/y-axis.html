How To Lie With Statistics

Perhaps the most famous book on statistics for the layperson is Darrell Huff's 1954 read, How to Lie with Statistics. The book was so-named due to the plentiful techniques the media has to distort, confuse, or otherwise mislead their audience with the use of true stastical data.

The media can lie with statistics by presenting polling data in a way that seems to indicate false conclusions.

The infamous Y-Axis

One of the most popular (and most simple) way to skew the significance of statistical findings is to present them in a graphical format with an unreasonable y-axis. By fiddling with the y-axis, news reporters can make the significant difference between two statistics appear to be greater, or lesser, depending on where they place the base of the y-axis.

Vocabulary

The y-axis is often referred to as the dependent variable in a formula. It is that which is explained by the x-axis, or independent variable. Shrinking the x-axis usually does not matter so much, because the x-axis is meant to categorize the real focus of our attention: the y-axis variable.
